Suppose a microscale lattice, \(x_j=j\) as in
\cref{fig:svhld}, has a property~\(u_j(t)\) that diffuses
with the period-two coefficients, named~\(a,b\):
\begin{equation}
\dot u_j=\begin{cases}
a[u_{j+1}-u_j]+b[u_{j-1}-u_j]&\text{odd }j,\\
b[u_{j+1}-u_j]+a[u_{j-1}-u_j]&\text{even }j.
\end{cases}
\label{eq:svhld}
\end{equation}
That is, the `diffusion' \ode{}s have the same \emph{form}
at every lattice point, but the coefficients of interaction
vary: they are periodic in~$n$ with period two (alternately
$a$~and~$b$ as shown in \cref{fig:svhld}). These \ode{}s
model the diffusion of quantity~$u$ through a microscale
laminate of alternating material and with alternating
diffusivities. The alternating diffusivities lead to fields
with a microscale jagged structure as seen in
\cref{fig:periodiidiff}. Our challenge is to deduce the
following effective macroscale diffusion equation for the
`smooth' component~\(U(x,t)\) of the field~$u$ over large
distances~$x$:
\begin{equation*}
\D tU \approx \frac{2ab}{a+b}\DD xU\,.
\end{equation*}

Let's form a system that is homogeneous on the lattice.
\cref{fig:dbl} shows how to do this by forming two decoupled
systems that we analyse as one whole system. Usefully, the
combined system is homogeneous on the lattice: every lattice
point has the same equations because diffusivity~\(a\)
always acts in the same direction, and the diffusivity~\(b\)
always acts in the other direction. The \ode{}s are
\begin{align*}&
\dot u_{1,j}=a[u_{2,j+1}-u_{1,j}]+b[u_{2,j-1}-u_{1,j}],
\\&
\dot u_{2,j}=b[u_{1,j+1}-u_{2,j}]+a[u_{1,j-1}-u_{2,j}].
\label{eq:}
\end{align*}
Writing the \ode{}s as in terms of the spatial location of
the lattice points, \(x=j\), the differential equations for
fields~\(u_1(x,t)\) and~\(u_2(x,t)\) are 
\begin{subequations}\label{eqssvhdmic}%
\begin{align}&
\dot u_1(x)=a[u_2(x+1)-u_1(x)] +b[u_2(x-1)-u_1(x)],
\\&
\dot u_2(x)=b[u_1(x+1)-u_2(x)] +a[u_1(x-1)-u_2(x)].
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
We analyse these to discover the homogenisation, and more.


The linearisation for \emph{gradual} variations in space is
to then neglect the spatial variations (\(u_j(x\pm1)\approx
u_j(x)\): \begin{equation*}
\D t{u_1}=(a+b)(u_2-u_1),\quad \D t{u_2}=(a+b)(u_1-u_2).
\end{equation*}
This pair of \ode{}s has eigenvalues \(\lambda=0,-2(a+b)\)
with respective eigenvectors \((1,1)\C (1,-1)\). We model
the effective `homogenised' dispersion of~\(u\) in
space-time over long times and large space scales.

Start by loading the procedure.
\begin{reduce}
in_tex "../invariantManifold.tex"$
\end{reduce}
In the printed output, group terms depending upon order of
spatial derivatives (which are assumed `small').
\begin{reduce}
factor small;
\end{reduce}
The following procedure call constructs the slow manifold
for this system to errors~\Ord{\partial_x^5}.  The spatial
finite differences are explicitly expanded in their
truncated Taylor series in partial derivatives:
\(u_j(x+h)=\sum_{n=0}^N h^n\Dn xn{u_j}/n!\).  The results
are clearer in mean and difference coefficients
\(c:=(a+b)/2\) and \(d:=(b-a)/2\).
\begin{reduce}
N:=4; % highest order of d/dx
a:=c-d; b:=c+d; % in mean and difference variables
if 0 then << %optional test of forcing
        depend F,x; depend f,x; let eps^2=>0; >>
    else F:=f:=0;
if 0 then << %optional test of uncertainty
        a:=a+small*epsilon; let eps^2=>0; >>;
factor df,diff;
invariantmanifold( {x},
    mat((-(a+b)*u1 +small*eps*(F+f)
        +a*(u2+ for n:=1:N sum pdf(u2,x,n)/factorial(n) )
        +b*(u2+ for n:=1:N sum pdf(u2,x,n)/factorial(n)*(-1)^n )
        ,-(a+b)*u2 +small*eps*(F-f)
        +b*(u1+ for n:=1:N sum pdf(u1,x,n)/factorial(n) )
        +a*(u1+ for n:=1:N sum pdf(u1,x,n)/factorial(n)*(-1)^n )
        )),
    mat(( 0 )), mat((1,1)), mat((1,1)),
    N+1 )$
end;
\end{reduce}


The procedure then actually analyses the parametrised system
\begin{align*}&
\D t{u_{1}}=\ParMath{
\frac{1}{12} \frac{\partial ^{4}u\_2}{\partial x^{4}} \eps^{4} c-\frac{1}{3} 
\frac{\partial ^{3}u\_2}{\partial x^{3}} \eps^{3} d+\frac{\partial ^{2}
u\_2}{\partial x^{2}} \eps^{2} c-2 \frac{\partial \,u\_2}{\partial \,x} 
\eps d-2 c u\_1+2 c u\_2\,,
}\\&
\D t{u_{2}}=\ParMath{
\frac{1}{12} \frac{\partial ^{4}u\_1}{\partial x^{4}} \eps^{4} c+\frac{1}{3} 
\frac{\partial ^{3}u\_1}{\partial x^{3}} \eps^{3} d+\frac{\partial ^{2}
u\_1}{\partial x^{2}} \eps^{2} c+2 \frac{\partial \,u\_1}{\partial \,x} 
\eps d+2 c u\_1-2 c u\_2\,.
}\end{align*}
Consequently the procedure's artificial `small'
parameter~\(\eps\)\ counts the number of spatial derivatives
in each term.


\paragraph{The invariant manifold} 
The slow manifold is expressed in terms of a series in space
derivatives.
\begin{align*}&
u_{1}=\ParMath{ \frac{\partial ^{3}s_{1}}{\partial x^{3}} \eps^{3} \big(\frac{1}{6} c^{-1}
 d-\frac{1}{8} c^{-3} d^{3}\big)-\frac{1}{2} \frac{\partial \,s_{1}}{\partial \,x} \eps 
c^{-1} d+O\big(\varepsilon ^{4}\big)+s_{1}
}\\&
u_{2}=\ParMath{ \frac{\partial ^{3}s_{1}}{\partial x^{3}} \eps^{3} \big(-\frac{1}{6} c^{-1
} d+\frac{1}{8} c^{-3} d^{3}\big)+\frac{1}{2} \frac{\partial \,s_{1}}{\partial \,x} \eps
 c^{-1} d+O\big(\varepsilon ^{4}\big)+s_{1}
}\end{align*}
That is, since \(u_1+u_2=s_1\)\,, \(s_1(x,t)\)~is a
`homogenised' mean variable~\(U(x,t)\).

 
\paragraph{Invariant manifold PDEs} 
The system evolves according to this \pde\ that describes
the effective dispersion of heat in the pipes: a simple
diffusion albeit with higher-order improvements:
\begin{align*}&
\D t{s_{1}}=\ParMath{ \frac{\partial ^{4}s_{1}}{\partial x^{4}} \eps^{4} \big(\frac{1}{12} 
c+\frac{1}{6} c^{-1} d^{2}-\frac{1}{4} c^{-3} d^{4}\big)+\frac{\partial ^{2}s_{1}}{
\partial x^{2}} \eps^{2} \big(c-c^{-1} d^{2}\big)+O\big(\varepsilon ^{5}
\big)
}\end{align*}
The second-order coefficient \(c-d^2/c=2ab/(a+b)\) as originally claimed.



\paragraph{Project initial conditions et al.}
To project initial conditions onto the slow manifold, or
non-autonomous forcing, or modifications of the original
system, or to quantify uncertainty \cite[Ch.12]{Roberts89b,
Roberts97b, Roberts2014a}, use the projection defined by the
following derived vector.
\begin{align*}&
\zv_1=\begin{bmatrix}z_{11}&z_{12}\end{bmatrix}^T
\\&
=\begin{bmatrix}
\ParMath{ \diff_{x}^{4} \eps^{4} \big(-\frac{1}{12} c^{-2} d^{2}+\frac{3}{32} c^{-4} d^{4}
\big)+\diff_{x}^{3} \eps^{3} \big(-\frac{1}{12} c^{-1} d+\frac{1}{8} c^{-3} d^{3}\big)+\frac{1}{8} \diff_{x}^{2} \eps^{2} c^{-2} d^{2}+\frac{1}{4} \diff_{x} \eps c^{-1} d+O
\big(\varepsilon ^{5}\big)+\frac{1}{2}
}\\[4ex]
\ParMath{ \diff_{x}^{4} \eps^{4} \big(-\frac{1}{12} c^{-2} d^{2}+\frac{3}{32} c^{-4} d^{4}
\big)+\diff_{x}^{3} \eps^{3} \big(\frac{1}{12} c^{-1} d-\frac{1}{8} c^{-3} d^{3}\big)+\frac{1}{8} \diff_{x}^{2} \eps^{2} c^{-2} d^{2}-\frac{1}{4} \diff_{x} \eps c^{-1} d+O\big(
\varepsilon ^{5}\big)+\frac{1}{2}
}
\end{bmatrix}.
\end{align*}


\paragraph{Forcing}
If we apply a forcing~\(f_1:=F(x,t)+f(x,t)\C
f_2:=F(x,t)-f(x,t))\) to the embedded
system~\cref{eqssvhdmic}, then the forcing is to be
projected onto the homogenised model using~\zv.  That is,
\begin{equation}
\D tU= (c-d^2/c)\DD xU +F +\frac d{2c}\D xf 
+\frac{d^2}{4c^2}\DD xF +\Ord{\partial_x^3}.
\label{EhomlatF}
\end{equation}
But we have only one physical strand of physical fields in
the specified problem.  The other strand of this embedding
is artificial.  Question: how do we distribute the forcing
between the physical and the artificial?

Tentative answer: the error~\Ord{\partial_x^3} contains
third derivatives of the fields.  I assume this error is
reasonably minimised by the fields in the two strands,
physical and artificial, are almost the same.   This
suggests we apply the same forcing to both strands,
\(f_1=f_2\), that is, the difference \(f=0\) which
consequently eliminates the term~\(+\frac d{2c}\D xf\) in
the above projection~\cref{EhomlatF}.

\paragraph{Uncertainty}  
The same projection applies to uncertainty
\cite[\S12.4]{Roberts2014a}.  Suppose the coefficients~\(a\)
are uncertain (and similar would apply to uncertain~\(b\)). 
Physically, \cref{fig:svhld}, the uncertain~\(a\) applies
between~\(u_{\text{odd}}\) on left and~\(u_{\text{even}}\)
on right.  Suppose one `smooth enough'
function~\(\epsilon(x,t)\) characterises the uncertainty for
both the physical field and the artificially introduced
field (\cref{fig:dbl}).  Then the governing differential
equations~\cref{eqssvhdmic} become
\begin{subequations}\label{EEsvhdmic}%
\begin{align}&
\dot u_1(x)=(a+\epsilon)[u_2(x+1)-u_1(x)] +b[u_2(x-1)-u_1(x)],
\\&
\dot u_2(x)=b[u_1(x+1)-u_2(x)] +(a+\epsilon)[u_1(x-1)-u_2(x)].
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
That is, the previously analysed system~\cref{eqssvhdmic}
with perturbing `forcing' \begin{align*}&
f_1:=\epsilon[u_2(x+1)-u_1(x)]=\epsilon[u_2-u_1+u_{2x}+u_{2xx}/2+\cdots],
\\&
f_2:=\epsilon[u_1(x-1)-u_2(x)]=\epsilon[u_1-u_2-u_{1x}+u_{1xx}/2+\cdots],
\\&\implies
F:=\epsilon\left[\tfrac12(u_{2x}-u_{1x})+\tfrac14(u_{1xx}+u_{2xx})+\cdots\right]
\approx \tfrac12\epsilon(\tfrac dc+1) U_{xx}\,,
\\&\qquad
f:=\epsilon[(u_2-u_1) +\tfrac12(u_{2x}+u_{1x}) +\tfrac14(u_{2xx}-u_{1xx})+\cdots]
\approx \epsilon(\tfrac dc+1) U_{x}\,,
\end{align*}
for the sum and difference, respectively. Consequently, this
uncertainty in coefficient~\(a\) modifies the homogenised
\pde~\cref{EhomlatF} to   
\begin{equation}
\D tU= (c-d^2/c)\DD xU +\tfrac12(\tfrac dc+1)\epsilon U_{xx} +\tfrac d{2c}(\tfrac dc+1)\D x{(\epsilon U_{x})} 
 +\Ord{\partial_x^3}.
\label{EhomlatUn}
\end{equation}
For constant~\(\epsilon\) this becomes
\begin{equation*}
\D tU= (c-d^2/c)\DD xU +\tfrac12(\tfrac dc+1)^2\epsilon U_{xx}  
 +\Ord{\partial_x^3},
\end{equation*}
in agreement with direct construction obtained by the
optional perturbation.
